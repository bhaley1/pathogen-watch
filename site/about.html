About Pathogen Watch

Foodborne illness in the United States is monitored by an ecosystem of overlapping systems — PulseNet, FoodNet, the FDA Coordinated Outbreak Response and Evaluation network, and the USDA Food Safety and Inspection Service among them. NCBI Pathogen Detection sits beneath all of them as the genomic reference: a nightly recomputation of SNP-based clusters across every Salmonella, STEC, Listeria, and Campylobacter isolate sequenced under public-health, regulatory, or research programs in any country.

NCBI's browser is excellent at finding a cluster you already know about. It is less useful for the inverse question: what changed overnight? Pathogen Watch exists to answer that question.

Audience

Epidemiologists, microbiologists, food-safety regulators, animal-health officials, public-health graduate students, and any scientist or journalist tracking human–animal–environmental linkages in foodborne pathogen genomics. No login is required to read; commenting requires only a free GitHub account.

What this is not

Pathogen Watch is not an official outbreak detection system, regulatory action trigger, or substitute for PulseNet, FoodNet, or any state or federal investigation. Cluster membership at NCBI Pathogen Detection is computed on the genomic data submitted to it; many investigations involve additional epidemiologic, environmental, and traceback evidence that does not appear here. Cluster events surfaced here may correspond to actively-investigated outbreaks, dormant historical clusters, sequencing-survey artifacts, or none of the above.
